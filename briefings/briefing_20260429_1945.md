## 3. NEW STORY UAE leaves OPEC
_Economy — importance 3.8 — 10 sources — latest reported 2026-04-29 16:44 UTC_

The United Arab Emirates’ decision to leave OPEC, announced Tuesday and analyzed widely on Wednesday, is a major blow to the oil cartel and to Saudi Arabia’s regional authority. The UAE is one of the group’s most important producers, and its departure weakens OPEC’s ability to coordinate supply at precisely the moment global energy markets are under strain from the Iran war and disruption around the Strait of Hormuz. Several outlets describe it as the most significant in a series of recent departures from the cartel.

The move is not only about the current war shock. The UAE has long objected to production quotas it believes constrained its capacity and exports, while Abu Dhabi has increasingly charted an independent foreign and economic policy distinct from Riyadh. Leaving OPEC gives it more freedom to pump on its own terms, potentially capture higher prices, and align more flexibly with customers and Washington. For Saudi Arabia, the loss of a key Gulf partner damages both market leverage and political prestige.

What to watch next is whether the UAE quickly raises output, whether OPEC+ can still enforce discipline among remaining members, and how oil prices respond. A weaker cartel could mean less ability to restrain price spikes or stabilize crashes, making outcomes less predictable for producers and consumers alike.
