Oil prices rose to their highest level in roughly four years on Wednesday as the crisis around the Strait of Hormuz deepened. Brent crude climbed to about $119 a barrel, matching or surpassing levels last seen at the start of the wider Middle East war. The immediate trigger was renewed concern that the US blockade of Iranian ports and Iran’s obstruction of traffic through the strait could persist, constraining a route that carried about a fifth of daily global oil consumption before the war.

The escalation is both economic and strategic. The Wall Street Journal, cited by NOS, reported that President Trump had instructed aides to prepare for an extended blockade, seeing it as a way to pressure Iran’s economy and nuclear policy without immediately resuming bombing. Al Jazeera reported Trump saying the naval siege was more effective than bombing, while Tehran threatened “practical” action. Other reports said shipping through Hormuz had been reduced to only a small number of vessels a day, while the New York Times noted Iranian threats to charge a toll for passage.

There are signs of strain on both sides. The Washington Post reported that the USS Gerald R. Ford is leaving the Middle East for repairs, reducing US firepower even as Washington presses Tehran. Humanitarian groups are calling for a corridor through the strait as aid deliveries are disrupted. Watch whether Iran’s offer to reopen Hormuz is revived, whether oil prices feed into inflation, and whether reduced US naval presence changes risk calculations.
